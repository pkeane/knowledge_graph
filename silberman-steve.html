Steve Silberman

1957 – 2024 · American
#psychology#disability#american-thought#neuroscience

American journalist and author, the writer who, more than any other single figure, brought the concept of neurodiversity to a general audience and placed it in its historical context. NeuroTribes: The Legacy of Autism and the Future of Neurodiversity (2015) — winner of the Samuel Johnson Prize for Non-Fiction — is the book that reframed autism and related neurological conditions not as diseases to be cured but as natural variations in human cognition that have always existed, that have contributed to human culture in ways the pathology model obscures, and that deserve recognition, accommodation, and respect rather than eradication.

Silberman was born in New York in 1957, studied with Allen Ginsberg at the Naropa Institute, and spent much of his career as a journalist at Wired magazine, where he covered technology, science, and culture. His 2001 Wired article "The Geek Syndrome" — about the apparent concentration of autism-spectrum conditions in the children of Silicon Valley engineers — was the seed of what became NeuroTribes. The article observed that in communities where systemizing, pattern-recognition, and intense focus were professionally rewarded, the traits associated with autism appeared at higher rates, raising the question of whether "autism" was a disease or the extreme end of a cognitive distribution that the tech industry happened to select for.

NeuroTribes (2015) expanded this into a full history. The book recovered the story of Hans Asperger's wartime clinic in Vienna — where Asperger, working under the shadow of the Nazi euthanasia program, argued that his "little professors" had valuable gifts — and set it against the very different American trajectory shaped by Leo Kanner, whose narrower definition of autism and whose emphasis on parental causation (the "refrigerator mother" theory) dominated American psychiatry for decades and led to institutionalization, behavioral punishment, and suffering on a vast scale. Silberman argued that the broadening of the autism diagnosis in the 1990s was not an "epidemic" but a belated correction — a recognition of the full spectrum of autistic cognition that Asperger had glimpsed in the 1940s.

The book's larger argument — that neurological diversity is a normal feature of human populations, not a collection of deficits to be corrected — has had influence well beyond autism. It gave the neurodiversity movement its historical grounding and its most accessible articulation, and it contributed to the broader shift in how ADHD, dyslexia, and other neurological variations are understood in education, employment, and public policy.

Silberman died in August 2024 at sixty-six.

Key ideas

  • Neurodiversity as a natural feature of human populations, not pathology
  • The hidden history of Hans Asperger and Leo Kanner — how diagnostic framing shaped the fate of autistic people
  • The "epidemic" as belated recognition — the broadening of the autism diagnosis as correction, not crisis
  • The relationship between cognitive variation and cultural contribution
  • The cost of the pathology model — institutionalization, behavioral punishment, exclusion
