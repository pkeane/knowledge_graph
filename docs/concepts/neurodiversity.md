# Neurodiversity

The proposition — advanced by the Australian sociologist Judy Singer in her 1998 thesis, given its major popular treatment in [[silberman-steve|Steve Silberman]]'s *NeuroTribes* (2015), and now increasingly adopted in psychology, education, and disability studies — that neurological variations such as autism, ADHD, dyslexia, and related conditions are natural features of human cognitive diversity rather than deficits or pathologies to be corrected. The neurodiversity paradigm (a term formalized by the philosopher Nick Walker) holds that there is no single "normal" brain; that the cognitive differences associated with these conditions carry genuine strengths alongside genuine difficulties; and that the appropriate social response is accommodation and recognition, not cure and normalization.

The concept has transformed the conversation in clinical psychology, education, and workplace policy over the past two decades. What it has *not* yet done — and this is the gap this page exists to name — is enter the mainstream of political philosophy, economic theory, or social theory in any sustained way.

## The gap in political and social theory

The observation that motivates this entry belongs to the graph's curator, not to the secondary literature:

> So much of political theory, economic theory, philosophical theory, even psychological theory assumes that "society" is relatively homogeneous — that people will behave rationally and generally in their own interests, motivated by the same human needs and longings, even if different theories weigh certain needs differently. But the neurodiversity paradigm suggests that this assumption is not a minor simplification but a foundational error. It is not that some people are "abnormal" or "pathological" versions of the standard rational agent — it is that there are genuinely different ways of perceiving, processing, and interacting with the world, and the canonical theories do not account for this.

This observation holds across the graph's major theoretical traditions:

- **The social contract.** From Hobbes through Locke through Rousseau through [[rawls-john|Rawls]], the contracting parties are assumed to be "roughly equal" in cognitive capacity and to reason in broadly similar ways. Rawls's "original position" imagines agents behind a veil of ignorance — but the rationality they exercise behind that veil is implicitly neurotypical: sequential, deliberative, consistent in preference-ordering. What happens to the social contract if the contracting parties include people whose executive function, sensory processing, or social cognition work on fundamentally different principles?

- **Communicative rationality.** [[habermas-jurgen|Habermas]]'s theory of communicative action assumes that the ideal speech situation involves participants who process discourse in roughly the same way — who can sustain linear argument, read pragmatic cues, and converge on consensus through a shared mode of reasoning. For many neurodiverse people, this describes an environment designed to exclude them.

- **Behavioral economics.** [[kahneman-daniel|Kahneman]] and Tversky's heuristics-and-biases program catalogs systematic deviations from rational-choice models — but treats them as *uniform* deviations, shared across the population. If cognitive processing differs structurally across neurotypes, then "System 1" and "System 2" may not describe a single architecture with predictable failure modes but several architectures with different ones.

- **Education.** [[dewey-john|Dewey]], [[freire-paulo|Freire]], and [[bruner-jerome|Bruner]] all assume a learner who can be reached through the right pedagogy — but the neurodiversity paradigm suggests that "the right pedagogy" may need to be plural in ways that go beyond learning-style preferences to genuine differences in how information is acquired, retained, and retrieved.

- **The utilitarian tradition.** If utility functions vary not just in content (what people want) but in *structure* (how people experience wanting, satisfaction, and decision), then the aggregation problems at the heart of utilitarian theory become harder than they already were.

## The mass-incarceration connection

The relationship between neurodiversity and [[mass-incarceration]] is documented and stark. Estimates put ADHD prevalence in U.S. prisons at 30–40 percent, compared to roughly 5 percent in the general population. Autism, traumatic brain injury, fetal alcohol spectrum disorder, and learning disabilities are all massively overrepresented in carceral populations. The mechanisms are multiple: impulsivity and executive-function differences increase the likelihood of behaviors that the criminal-justice system punishes; difficulty reading social cues and responding to authority in expected ways leads to escalation at every stage from police encounter through sentencing; the system's procedural demands — sitting still, maintaining eye contact, following complex proceedings, demonstrating "appropriate" affect — are themselves neurotypical norms enforced as legal requirements. To the extent that mass incarceration is partly a system for managing people whose cognition does not conform to social expectations, the neurodiversity paradigm reframes it as a civil-rights problem of a kind the existing literature has not fully named.

## Who is closest to this work

No single thinker has produced the full synthesis. The figures who come nearest:

- **[[nussbaum-martha|Martha Nussbaum]]** — *Frontiers of Justice* (2006) explicitly extends the capabilities approach to people with cognitive disabilities, arguing that Rawlsian contractarianism cannot accommodate them because it assumes "roughly equal" parties. This is the most serious attempt by a major political philosopher to think beyond the neurotypical subject.

- **[[foucault-michel|Michel Foucault]]** — *Madness and Civilization* (1961) remains the foundational account of how Western society has constructed cognitive and psychological difference as pathology requiring confinement. The neurodiversity paradigm can be read as a Foucauldian project: denaturalizing the line between "normal" and "disordered" cognition.

- **[[laing-rd|R. D. Laing]]** — the anti-psychiatry movement's insistence that "schizophrenia" might be a comprehensible response to an incomprehensible situation anticipated the neurodiversity paradigm's refusal to treat difference as disease, though Laing's framework was existential-phenomenological rather than cognitive.

- **Alasdair MacIntyre** — *Dependent Rational Animals* (1999) argues that vulnerability and dependence are central features of human life, not edge cases to be handled after the "normal" theory is built.

- **Eva Feder Kittay** — *Love's Labor: Essays on Women, Equality, and Dependency* (1999) argues that the liberal tradition's emphasis on independence and autonomy is built on a false picture of what human life actually looks like, a picture that excludes her daughter and millions of others with cognitive disabilities.

- **Ian Hacking** — *The Social Construction of What?* (1999) and his work on "making up people" examines how diagnostic categories do not merely describe but *create* kinds of people — directly relevant to how "ADHD" and "autism" function as social as well as clinical categories.

- **[[silberman-steve|Steve Silberman]]** — *NeuroTribes* (2015) is the major popular history and the most accessible articulation of the neurodiversity paradigm. It gave the movement its historical grounding but does not attempt the political-theoretical synthesis.

## The missing book

The book that would take the neurodiversity paradigm seriously as a challenge to the foundations of Western political and social theory — not as a disability-rights addendum to existing frameworks but as a revision of the assumptions on which those frameworks rest — has not been written. The pieces are in place: Nussbaum's capabilities work, Foucault's archaeology of normality, the empirical literature on neurodivergent overrepresentation in the criminal-justice system, and the neurodiversity movement's own insistence that this is not about accommodation within existing structures but about rethinking what the structures assume. This page exists to mark the gap and to note that it is ripe for further investigation.
