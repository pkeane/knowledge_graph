# Lawrence Ferlinghetti

American poet, painter, publisher, bookseller, and the co-founder of City Lights Books in San Francisco; the indispensable institutional figure of the Beat generation and, through the publication of [[ginsberg-allen|Allen Ginsberg]]'s *Howl* and the 1957 obscenity trial that followed, the person most directly responsible for establishing the legal and cultural ground on which postwar American poetry could say what it wanted to say.

Born in Yonkers, New York in 1919, raised through a fractured early childhood partly in France and partly by relatives in Bronxville, Ferlinghetti served as a Navy officer during the Second World War — he was at Normandy as a submarine-chaser commander and visited Nagasaki six weeks after the atomic bombing, an experience he later said permanently shaped his politics — took a BA at the University of North Carolina, an MA at Columbia, and a doctorate at the Sorbonne. He settled in San Francisco in 1951, and in 1953, with Peter D. Martin, opened City Lights as the first all-paperback bookstore in the United States, on Columbus Avenue in North Beach, where it has operated continuously ever since.

The City Lights Pocket Poets Series, which Ferlinghetti launched in 1955, made small-format, inexpensive paperbacks the primary vehicle for a new American poetry. The fourth title in the series, Ginsberg's *Howl and Other Poems* (1956), was seized by U.S. Customs in 1957 on obscenity grounds; San Francisco police subsequently arrested Ferlinghetti and his manager Shigeyoshi Murao. Ferlinghetti was defended by the ACLU, Judge Clayton Horn ruled that the book had "redeeming social importance" and was therefore constitutionally protected, and the precedent the trial set — *People v. Ferlinghetti* (1957) — is a standard reference in First Amendment obscenity law.

Ferlinghetti's own poetry, more traditional than Ginsberg's or Kerouac's, drew on French surrealism, e. e. cummings, and the long Whitmanian line. *A Coney Island of the Mind* (1958) has sold more than a million copies — among the highest-selling American poetry books of the twentieth century — and has remained continuously in print. *Her* (1960), a surrealist novel, and a long sequence of later collections followed. He was named San Francisco's first Poet Laureate in 1998. He continued to paint until late in life, with a sustained exhibition record, and he directed the City Lights bookstore and publishing house until his death at 101 in 2021.

## Key ideas

- The poet as public speaker rather than specialist
- The small independent press and bookstore as the indispensable institutional base for non-commercial literature
- Poetry's political task as "a lifetime of insurgency" against conformist pieties
- Continuity with Whitman's democratic line and with European surrealist freedom of image
