# David Hume

The sharpest of the British empiricists and the philosopher who, in Kant's famous phrase, woke him from his dogmatic slumber. Hume pushed [[locke-john]]'s empiricism to its unsettling conclusions: if all knowledge comes from experience, our belief in causation is just habit — we see billiard balls strike and move, never "cause" itself. The self is a bundle of perceptions, not a substance. Induction has no rational foundation. And crucially for ethics: you cannot derive an *ought* from an *is*.

The last point is the famous "Hume's guillotine," and it frames most subsequent moral philosophy. Hume's own answer was that morality rests on sentiment — sympathy, approval, disapproval — not reason. Reason, he wrote, "is and ought only to be the slave of the passions." This naturalistic picture of ethics fed directly into his close friend [[smith-adam]]'s *Theory of Moral Sentiments* and, more distantly, into [[bentham-jeremy]]'s utilitarianism.

Politically Hume was a cautious Tory skeptic of [[social-contract]] theory: government, he argued, rests on habit, interest, and convention, not on any original agreement. He also wrote a bestselling *History of England* and quietly demolished the rational case for miracles — earning him enough enmity that the university chairs he wanted were closed to him. He died cheerfully unrepentant, to the dismay of his religious contemporaries.

## Key ideas

- The problem of induction
- Is/ought distinction ("Hume's guillotine")
- Morality grounded in sentiment, not reason
- The self as a bundle of perceptions

## Key works

- *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–40)
- *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding* (1748)
- *An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals* (1751)
- *Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion* (posthumous, 1779)

## Secondary sources

- Ernest Campbell Mossner, *The Life of David Hume* (1954; 2nd ed. 1980) — the standard biography; long unsurpassed.
- James A. Harris, *Hume: An Intellectual Biography* (2015) — the outstanding modern life; reads Hume as a man of letters rather than principally as an academic philosopher.
- Barry Stroud, *Hume* (1977) — the best short philosophical introduction in English.
