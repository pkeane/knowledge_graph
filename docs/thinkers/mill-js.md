# John Stuart Mill

The central figure of 19th-century English [[classical-liberalism]] and the thinker who made [[utilitarianism]] philosophically respectable by widening it past the narrow hedonism of his godfather [[bentham-jeremy]]. Raised in a famously rigorous home-schooling regime by his father James Mill, he suffered a mental crisis in his twenties which he credited to an upbringing that neglected feeling — a crisis resolved, in part, by reading Wordsworth.

*On Liberty* (1859) is his most enduring statement: the "harm principle" — that the only justification for coercing an individual is to prevent harm to others — remains the touchstone of liberal argument about free speech, lifestyle, and state power. *Utilitarianism* (1861) distinguishes higher and lower pleasures, trying to rescue the doctrine from the charge that it reduces ethics to pig-satisfaction. *The Subjection of Women* (1869), written with and deeply shaped by his wife [[taylor-mill-harriet]], is one of the foundational texts of liberal feminism.

Late Mill drifted toward a cautious socialism, worried that unregulated capitalism threatened the very individuality his liberalism prized.

## Key ideas

- The harm principle
- Higher vs. lower pleasures
- Liberty of thought and discussion as epistemically necessary
- Representative government with safeguards against majority tyranny
