# David Lay Williams

American political theorist, Professor of Political Science at DePaul University, and — with the 2024 publication of *The Greatest of All Plagues: How Economic Inequality Shaped Political Thought from Plato to Marx* (Princeton) — the scholar who has made the strongest recent case that concern about economic inequality is not a modern left-wing preoccupation but a through-line of the Western political canon. Williams's earlier scholarship (*Rousseau's Platonic Enlightenment*, 2007; *Rousseau's *Social Contract*: An Introduction*, 2014) established him as a serious Rousseau and early-modern specialist; *The Greatest of All Plagues* extends that historical expertise into a book-length intervention in contemporary debate.

The argument, pursued across dedicated chapters on Plato, Jesus of Nazareth, [[hobbes-thomas|Hobbes]], [[rousseau-jj|Rousseau]], [[smith-adam|Adam Smith]], [[mill-js|J.S. Mill]], and [[marx-karl|Marx]], is that each of these canonical figures treated the unequal distribution of wealth as a primary political danger — not a secondary economic matter — and that the 20th-century habit of bracketing inequality from "real" political theory is itself the historical anomaly. Plato's *Laws* sets a ratio beyond which no citizen's holdings should exceed the poorest's; the Gospel's Jesus is consistently hostile to accumulated wealth; Hobbes worries about faction rooted in economic resentment; Rousseau treats inequality as the origin of political corruption; even Smith — usually conscripted into arguments for minimal government — devotes sustained attention to the political dangers of concentrated wealth. Williams writes as a historian of political thought rather than a polemicist, but the cumulative effect is polemical: to supply the intellectual-history backbone that contemporary writers like [[reich-robert|Reich]], [[hacker-jacob|Hacker]] and [[pierson-paul|Pierson]], and [[piketty-thomas|Piketty]] typically presuppose but rarely provide.

## Key ideas

- Economic inequality as canonical, not peripheral, political concern
- The bracketing of distributive questions as a 20th-century anomaly
- Close historical reading in service of contemporary argument
- Rousseau's political thought as inequality-centered
