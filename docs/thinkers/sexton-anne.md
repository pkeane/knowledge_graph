# Anne Sexton

American poet who came to writing in her late twenties on the recommendation of her psychiatrist, after a postpartum breakdown and a suicide attempt, and who in less than two decades produced eight books and a Pulitzer Prize before her own suicide in 1974. Sexton studied with [[lowell-robert|Lowell]] at Boston University in 1958–1959; [[plath-sylvia|Plath]] was in the same seminar; the three of them, with [[berryman-john|Berryman]], became the central figures of what came to be called the confessional school.

The early books — *To Bedlam and Part Way Back* (1960), *All My Pretty Ones* (1962), *Live or Die* (1966, Pulitzer) — drew directly on her hospitalizations, her relationship to her parents, her difficulties as a mother, her abortions, her body, and her sex life, in a register that was unusual for the time in its directness and unusual at any time in its formal control. *Transformations* (1971), Sexton's reworking of seventeen Grimm fairy tales, is among her most original books and the one her late readers have tended to elevate.

The late religious work — *The Awful Rowing Toward God* (1975, posthumous), and the unfinished *Words for Dr. Y.* — records a search for a personal God that did not arrive in time. Sexton's reception suffered for some decades from the assumption that confessional poetry was inherently exhibitionistic; the recovery of her work since the 1990s has tended to emphasize its formal intelligence and its place in the feminist reading of the female lyric.

## Key themes

- The female body and its medical management
- Mental illness and the psychiatric encounter
- Fairy tale as feminist archive
- The search for God as the late subject

## Key works

- *All My Pretty Ones* (1962)
- *Live or Die* (1966) — Pulitzer
- *Love Poems* (1969)
- *Transformations* (1971)

## Secondary sources

- Diane Wood Middlebrook, *Anne Sexton: A Biography* (1991) — the standard life; controversial for its use of psychiatric tape recordings released to the biographer by Sexton's psychiatrist.
- Linda Gray Sexton, *Searching for Mercy Street: My Journey Back to My Mother, Anne Sexton* (1994) — the daughter's memoir, including the abuse the biography first disclosed.
- Helen Vendler, "Anne Sexton" (in *The Music of What Happens*, 1988).
- Caroline King Barnard Hall, *Anne Sexton* (1989) — the standard short critical study.
