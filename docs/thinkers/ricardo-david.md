# David Ricardo

The most rigorous of the classical political economists — a self-made stockbroker who retired at 42, taught himself economics, and produced a theoretical framework that shaped 19th-century economic argument from [[mill-js]] to [[marx-karl]]. Where [[smith-adam]] wrote in broad, morally-textured paragraphs, Ricardo thought in abstract models.

Two ideas carried farthest. His *theory of comparative advantage* (1817) showed that even a country less productive at everything still benefits from specializing in what it makes *relatively* best and trading for the rest — the single most durable argument for free trade ever made, and still the core of international economics. His *labor theory of value* held that the exchange value of goods is determined, under competitive conditions, by the labor required to produce them. Ricardo deployed this descriptively; [[marx-karl]] would later turn the same apparatus into a moral indictment of capitalism.

His *Principles of Political Economy and Taxation* (1817) also developed a grim account of distribution. As population grew, Ricardo argued, ever-poorer land would have to be cultivated, rents would rise to landlords, wages would drift toward subsistence, and profits would be squeezed — a secular crisis he thought only free trade in grain could postpone. This analysis armed Richard Cobden's campaign against the Corn Laws, which were repealed two decades after Ricardo's death.

## Key ideas

- Comparative advantage
- Labor theory of value (classical version)
- Differential rent and the tendency of profit to fall
- Iron law of wages
