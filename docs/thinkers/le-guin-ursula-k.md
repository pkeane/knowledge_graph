# Ursula K. Le Guin

American novelist whose work inside the marginalized commercial genres of science fiction and fantasy built — across twenty-three novels, a dozen story collections, and six decades — one of the most politically and philosophically serious bodies of fiction produced by any American writer of her generation. Daughter of the anthropologist Alfred L. Kroeber and the writer Theodora Kroeber (whose *Ishi in Two Worlds* is the life of the last Yahi man her father studied), Le Guin carried the anthropological sensibility into fiction that took seriously the idea that human social arrangements are varied, historical, and could be otherwise.

*The Left Hand of Darkness* (1969) imagines a planet whose inhabitants are genderless except during a monthly estrus in which any individual can become either male or female — a thought experiment that feminist science fiction has never quite exhausted. *The Dispossessed* (1974), subtitled "An Ambiguous Utopia," is the great modern anarchist novel, setting an austere anarcho-syndicalist moon against the consumer-capitalist and state-socialist planet it orbits, and refusing to let either side of the comparison off. The Earthsea sequence (*A Wizard of Earthsea*, 1968; *The Tombs of Atuan*, 1971; *The Farthest Shore*, 1972; and — twenty years later, reconsidering the patriarchal bones of her own youthful fantasy — *Tehanu*, 1990, and after) is among the great achievements of modern fantasy.

Le Guin was a lifelong reader of [[lao-tzu|Laozi]] (her working version of the *Tao Te Ching*, 1997, is the culmination of decades of study) and of Kropotkin, whose mutual-aid anarchism is the political ground of *The Dispossessed*. Her 2014 National Book Foundation speech — "We live in capitalism. Its power seems inescapable. So did the divine right of kings" — went viral in an industry not given to such statements, and remains the clearest short statement of her politics. Her essays, collected in *The Language of the Night* (1979), *Dancing at the Edge of the World* (1989), and *No Time to Spare* (2017), argue across forty years for the dignity of imaginative literature and for the anthropological seriousness of forms the literary establishment had dismissed.

## Key ideas

- Science fiction as thought-experiment — social, political, anthropological
- Anarchism as a realistically imagined social form (*The Dispossessed*)
- Taoism as philosophical ground
- Gender as contingent, not natural
- The defense of genre fiction as literature

## Secondary sources

- Julie Phillips, *Ursula K. Le Guin: A Writer's Life* (forthcoming; interim essays 2016–present) — the authorized biography; Phillips had Le Guin's cooperation.
- Charlotte Spivack, *Ursula K. Le Guin* (1984) — the Twayne-series introductory critical study.
- Harold Bloom, ed., *Ursula K. Le Guin: Modern Critical Views* (1986, rev. 2000) — the standard critical anthology.
- Warren G. Rochelle, *Communities of the Heart: The Rhetoric of Myth in the Fiction of Ursula K. Le Guin* (2001) — the best full-length study of her politics and poetics together.
- David Naimon, *Conversations on Writing* (2018) — the late conversations; as close as we have to a Le Guin ars poetica.
