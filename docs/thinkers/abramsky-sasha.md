# Sasha Abramsky

British-American journalist, *Nation* contributor, and lecturer at UC Davis whose long-form reporting has worked steadily across two of the most consequential and least telegenic American subjects: incarceration and poverty. Abramsky comes out of an English left-intellectual family — grandson of the historian Chimen Abramsky — and studied at Oxford and Columbia before settling in Sacramento.

*Hard Time Blues* (2002) and *American Furies* (2007) traced the American prison boom — the three-strikes laws, the spread of supermax confinement, the political economy of a country that incarcerates more of its people than any other on earth. *The American Way of Poverty: How the Other Half Still Lives* (2013), explicitly conceived as a 21st-century successor to Michael Harrington's *The Other America*, is his most ambitious book — a several-year reporting project across dozens of states documenting the lives of the roughly 50 million Americans living below or near the poverty line, paired with a concrete policy program for what a renewed American social-democratic settlement could look like.

His later books include *The House of Twenty Thousand Books* (2014), a memoir of his grandfather's London home and library — an unexpected, lovely book on Jewish socialist intellectual culture in the 20th century — and *Jumping at Shadows* (2017), on the gap between the actual decline of American violent crime since the early 1990s and the political and media climate of escalating fear that has accompanied it. *Little Wonder* (2020) profiled the Norwegian runner Greta Andersen and the early-20th-century woman runner who inspired her. He writes a regular *Nation* column on California politics and the carceral state.

## Key themes

- The American carceral state as a structural fact, not an aberration
- Poverty as policy choice, not personal failure
- The political uses of fear in declining-crime America
- Renewing the American social-democratic tradition
- Jewish left intellectual inheritance
