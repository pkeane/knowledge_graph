# A Reading List

Books that match the values lens of this knowledge graph and (where possible) connect to nodes already in it. A mix of essential classics you may not have read and recent works that synthesize the past well.

## Six classics, each short

1. **[[thurman-howard|Howard Thurman]], *Jesus and the Disinherited*** (1949) — under 100 pages. The single book that maps closest to your VALUES; MLK reportedly carried it on the road. If you read only one thing on this list, this one.
2. **[[du-bois-web|W.E.B. Du Bois]], *The Souls of Black Folk*** (1903) — fourteen essays mixing sociology, memoir, and lyric meditation. Foundation of American Black political thought; the chapter on the sorrow songs is a music essay you'll never forget.
3. **[[baldwin-james|James Baldwin]], *The Fire Next Time*** (1963) — two essays, ~120 pages. Devastatingly clear on race and on what White American Christianity has refused to see. Reads in one sitting.
4. **[[niebuhr-reinhold|Reinhold Niebuhr]], *The Irony of American History*** (1952) — the canonical Christian-realist statement on good and evil intertwined. Short, hard, durable.
5. **[[berry-wendell|Wendell Berry]], *Hannah Coulter*** (2004) — short novel. Berry's agrarian Christianity in narrative rather than essay form; quieter than the essays and easier to enter through.
6. **Marilynne Robinson, *Gilead*** (2004) — Pulitzer; a small American masterpiece. An aging Iowa Congregationalist preacher writing to his young son. Calvinist humanism rendered as fiction. *(Robinson is on the wishlist but not yet in the graph.)*

## Five recent works that synthesize well

7. **[[stevenson-bryan|Bryan Stevenson]], *Just Mercy*** (2014) — the indispensable contemporary book on the American carceral state. Built around the Walter McMillian case; in a Sermon-on-the-Mount lineage but by way of Alabama death row.
8. **[[wilkerson-isabel|Isabel Wilkerson]], *The Warmth of Other Suns*** (2010) — fifteen years of reporting on the Great Migration through three life stories. One of the best American nonfiction books of the century, on a subject most American history misses.
9. **[[mazzucato-mariana|Mariana Mazzucato]], *The Entrepreneurial State*** (2013) — short and polemical. Will permanently change how you read the Silicon Valley story.
10. **[[anderson-elizabeth|Elizabeth Anderson]], *Private Government*** (2017) — about 100 pages of lectures plus respondents. The argument that the modern American workplace is *de facto* authoritarian and that classical liberalism, properly understood, ought to be horrified.
11. **Heather McGhee, *The Sum of Us*** (2021) — accessible, vivid, the single best contemporary book on how racism costs everyone (the "drained pool" framing). Reads like long-form journalism; argues like serious political analysis. *(McGhee is not yet in the graph.)*

## One wildcard worth your attention

12. **Adam Tooze, *Crashed*** (2018) — 700 pages on the 2008 financial crisis and the decade after; the most ambitious recent attempt to write the political economy of crisis as a single coherent story. Hard going but worth it; it's the book to read after [[stiglitz-joseph|Stiglitz]], [[krugman-paul|Krugman]], and [[minsky-hyman|Minsky]] to see how the crisis actually unfolded across the world economy. *(Tooze is not yet in the graph.)*

## Fiction — in the same values-cluster

Novels (and stories) that do the philosophical and political work of the graph in another idiom. The two novels listed in the classics section above ([[berry-wendell|Berry's]] *Hannah Coulter*, Marilynne Robinson's *Gilead*) also belong here.

13. **[[percy-walker|Walker Percy]], *The Moviegoer*** (1961) — National Book Award. Binx Bolling, a New Orleans stockbroker, living in what Percy called "everydayness" and undertaking a quiet, Kierkegaardian search he can't quite name. One of the clearest American expressions of existentialist themes in fiction; a short, strange, funny, permanent book.
14. **[[davies-robertson|Robertson Davies]], *The Manticore*** (1972) — the second of the Deptford Trilogy. A Canadian lawyer undergoes Jungian analysis in Zurich after his father's violent death; the novel is the analysis, rendered with unusual technical accuracy. Best read after *Fifth Business* (1970), which sets up the world, but can stand alone.
15. **[[o-connor-flannery|Flannery O'Connor]], *A Good Man Is Hard to Find*** (1955) — short stories. O'Connor's Catholic imagination in its compressed, violent, unforgettable form. Grace as intrusion rather than comfort.
16. **[[morrison-toni|Toni Morrison]], *Beloved*** (1987) — Pulitzer. Built around the historical case of Margaret Garner. The great American novel of the afterlives of slavery; formally daring, ethically unsparing. By broad consensus one of the major American novels of the 20th century.
17. **[[ellison-ralph|Ralph Ellison]], *Invisible Man*** (1952) — mid-century American masterpiece. The unnamed Black narrator's picaresque from the South through Harlem; prose that never settles into one register for long. Among the handful of essential postwar American novels.
18. **[[baldwin-james|James Baldwin]], *Go Tell It on the Mountain*** (1953) — Baldwin's first novel, semi-autobiographical, set in a Harlem Pentecostal church across one Saturday night. The novel in which his later essayistic concerns — Black Christianity, the father, inherited sin, the claim of love — first take full form.
19. **[[woolf-virginia|Virginia Woolf]], *To the Lighthouse*** (1927) — her formal masterpiece. The middle section "Time Passes" — war, death, and the decay of the summer house recorded in ten elliptical pages between two domestic scenes — is one of the great experiments of modernist prose.
20. **[[faulkner-william|William Faulkner]], *Absalom, Absalom!*** (1936) — Faulkner's most ambitious book. Four narrators try to make sense of Thomas Sutpen's rise and ruin in antebellum Mississippi. The great American novel of how the past will not stay past; race, memory, and inheritance as the subject matter of the country itself.
21. **[[camus-albert|Albert Camus]], *The Plague*** (1947) — a plague descends on Oran and the book tests, unsentimentally and without theological consolation, what decency looks like under conditions that admit no triumph. Camus's most durable work of fiction.
22. **[[el-akkad-omar|Omar El Akkad]], *What Strange Paradise*** (2021) — Giller Prize. Short alternating chapters between a drowning refugee boat and the small island receiving its survivors. Contemporary, sharply made, morally unflinching.
23. **[[didion-joan|Joan Didion]], *Play It As It Lays*** (1970) — her best novel. California, anomie, the hollowed-out interior life of a particular American 1960s. Short, exact, terrifying in the way Didion at her best is terrifying.

## A one-month plan

If you want a focused start: Thurman → Baldwin → Niebuhr → Stevenson → McGhee. Five short books, total under a thousand pages, that together rehearse most of your VALUES.md from inside the traditions that hold them.

For a parallel month in fiction: Percy's *Moviegoer* → O'Connor's stories → Baldwin's *Go Tell It on the Mountain* → Morrison's *Beloved* → Davies' *Fifth Business* + *The Manticore*. Same commitments, rendered as narrative.
