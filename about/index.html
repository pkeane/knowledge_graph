About the Knowledge Graph

How it was made

This knowledge graph was generated by Claude Code, Anthropic's command-line coding assistant, at the prompting of Peter Keane. Peter chose the thinkers, set the scope, and directed revisions; Claude Code produced the entries, the cross-links, the build scripts, and the site.

The content is received wisdom, not creative thought. Each entry summarizes and synthesizes existing scholarly and critical consensus — standard biographies, conventional interpretations, well-known ideas and works — as represented in the language model's training data. The graph does not contain original research, independent interpretation, or creative argument by either the user or the model.

No entry has been independently fact-checked, and all errors are the product of a statistical language model working from its training data. Readers should treat entries as starting points, not authorities, and should verify particulars before citing them.

Scope

The core of the graph is Western political, social, economic, and philosophical thought from roughly the 18th century to the present — the liberal, Marxist, conservative, and religious traditions; moral philosophy and social theory; the American civil-rights and feminist traditions; the critique of capitalism and the critics of that critique.

From that core the graph extends along several lines:

  • Non-Western political, religious, and literary thought that has shaped the modern world: Gandhi, Ambedkar, Césaire, Fanon, Ngũgĩ, Achebe, Soyinka, Tagore, Confucius, Ibn Khaldun, Mao, Mariátegui, Shariati, Darwish, Endō, Roy.
  • Literature, poetry, and drama where the work carries political, social, philosophical, or theological weight — novelists (Tolstoy, Dostoevsky, George Eliot, García Márquez, Morrison, Baldwin, Ellison, Coetzee, Gordimer, Lessing, Le Guin), poets (Whitman, Dickinson, Yeats, Hughes, Rich, Heaney, Neruda, Lorca, Akhmatova, Dylan), playwrights (Beckett, Havel, Soyinka).
  • Design, architecture, and urbanism where they carry an articulated social program — Morris and the Arts and Crafts movement, the Bauhaus circle (Gropius, Moholy-Nagy, Albers, Klee, Kandinsky), Mumford, Jane Jacobs, Christopher Alexander.
  • Psychology and psychoanalysis where it has shaped political and moral thought — Freud, Jung, Erikson.

The graph is not an encyclopedia. Coverage is selective and reflects the interests that directed its construction.

Commitments that shape the selection

The selection is not neutral. The following commitments are visible in which thinkers receive entries, which lineages receive greater density, and which secondary sources are recommended.

Political economy

  • Economic inequality is treated as a threat to democratic self-government, on the argument that concentrated wealth translates into concentrated political voice. Entries on Reich, Hacker and Pierson, Piketty, Stiglitz, Giridharadas, Anderson, Polanyi, Keynes, and Galbraith reflect this emphasis.
  • Regulated capitalism is treated as the default position, rather than laissez-faire or state socialism. The libertarian tradition (Hayek, Friedman, Nozick) is included because it is the position the graph's center of gravity is arguing against.
  • A strong public sector, including digital public infrastructure, is treated as a legitimate and underdeveloped subject.
  • The "racial capitalism" line — Cedric Robinson, Du Bois, Wilkerson, Davis — is treated as part of the political-economic core rather than a separate subfield.

Moral and religious life

  • The Black American intellectual tradition is weighted heavily: Douglass, Du Bois, Wells, Thurman, Baldwin, Ellison, Morrison, Murray, Lorde, Davis, Coates, Cone, West, Alexander, Stevenson.
  • Feminist thought is represented across generations: Wollstonecraft, de Beauvoir, Woolf, hooks, Lorde, Rich, Young, Crenshaw (as concept), Nussbaum.
  • Radical and social-gospel Christianity is weighted over confessional or evangelical currents: Day, Merton, Bonhoeffer, Thurman, King, Romero, Gutiérrez, Stevenson, Berry, Rauschenbusch.
  • The pluralist and tragic lineage — Niebuhr, Berlin, Bernard Williams, Arendt, Flannery O'Connor — is preferred to systematizing moral theories.
  • Existentialist and religious-existentialist writers are weighted over essentialist ones: Kierkegaard, Nietzsche, Heidegger, Sartre, de Beauvoir, Camus, Tillich, Unamuno, Percy, Endō.

Arts as thought

Literature, poetry, drama, and design are treated as forms in which a culture does political, social, and philosophical work, not as decoration. Writers and artists receive entries on the same footing as political theorists where their work is engaged with these questions.

Biases to be aware of

  • American emphasis, on the non-European side. American political and religious material is denser than any other national tradition.
  • Christian emphasis, on the religious side. Christianity is more thoroughly covered than Judaism, Islam, or the Asian traditions; within Christianity, the radical, social-gospel, liberation-theology, and contemplative lines are weighted over the evangelical or confessional ones.
  • Left-of-center, in the American sense. Conservative thinkers of substantial weight (MacIntyre, Burke, Oakeshott, parts of Niebuhr, Scruton) are included, but the center of gravity is to the left.
  • Post-1800 emphasis. Ancient and medieval thinkers appear where required (Aristotle, Confucius, Ibn Khaldun) but are the exception. The graph is primarily a map of modernity and its immediate prehistory.
  • Historical over contemporary. Living thinkers are less fully represented than historical ones, on the grounds that unfinished careers are harder to characterize.

Structure of an entry

Most entries follow the same short form: a YAML header (dates, nationality, tags, cross-references), two to four paragraphs of orientation, a list of key ideas, a list of key works, and a few named secondary sources. Entries are cross-linked by influenced_by, influenced, and related fields. The cross-links are often more informative than the tags for navigation.

Corrections

Errors and omissions are expected. Corrections can be submitted to the GitHub repository at github.com/pkeane/kg.
