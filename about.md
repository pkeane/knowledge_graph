# About the Knowledge Graph

A personal, opinionated map of the thinkers, writers, artists, and movements that have shaped how I try to understand the world. Each entry is short — a paragraph or two of orientation, a list of key ideas and works, and a few secondary sources for anyone who wants to go further. The entries are cross-linked by who influenced whom and who argued with whom, so that browsing by link tends to surface the conversations rather than the isolated figures.

## Territory

The core is **Western political, social, economic, and philosophical thought** from roughly the 18th century to the present. That's the part of the graph with the densest coverage — the liberal, Marxist, conservative, and religious traditions and their quarrels with each other; the moral-philosophical and social-theoretic currents; the American civil-rights and feminist traditions; the critique of capitalism, and of the critics of the critique.

From that core the graph branches out along a few lines where I've found the conversations I care about don't stay bounded:

- **Non-Western political and religious thought** that shaped the modern world: Gandhi, Ambedkar, Césaire, Fanon, Ngũgĩ, Achebe, Tagore, Confucius, Ibn Khaldun, Mao, Mariátegui, Shariati, Soyinka, Darwish, Roy. These aren't a token appendix — the argument of the graph is that a "Western" map of modernity that leaves these figures out is the wrong map.
- **Literature, poetry, and drama** — wherever the work is doing political, social, philosophical, or theological work. The novelists (Tolstoy, Dostoevsky, Eliot, García Márquez, Morrison, Baldwin, Ellison, Coetzee, Gordimer, Lessing), the poets (Whitman, Dickinson, Yeats, Hughes, Rich, Heaney, Neruda, Lorca, Akhmatova, Dylan), the playwrights (Beckett, Havel, Soyinka). Where a writer's work is primarily aesthetic with little political or social charge, they are outside the scope.
- **The arts more broadly** where they carry an articulated social program — Morris and the Arts and Crafts movement, the Bauhaus, Mumford and Alexander in urbanism.
- **Psychology and psychoanalysis** where they've shaped political and moral thought — Freud, Jung, Erikson.

The graph is **not** an encyclopedia. I'm not trying to cover everything; I'm trying to cover the conversations I want to be able to re-enter.

## Values behind the selection

The graph has a point of view. The selection tilts toward the following commitments, and the reader should know it.

### On political economy

- **Wealth inequality is itself a threat to democracy.** Money buys political voice; political voice cements the arrangements that produce more wealth. I take the vicious cycle to be the single most important political fact of the present moment. The graph is accordingly dense on Reich, Hacker and Pierson, Piketty, Stiglitz, Giridharadas, Anderson, Polanyi, Keynes, Galbraith.
- **Regulated capitalism**, not laissez-faire and not state socialism. Markets are useful instruments; they are not self-justifying and they are not self-regulating. The graph includes the libertarian tradition (Hayek, Friedman, Nozick) because I think it is important to know what one is arguing with, not because I agree with it.
- **A strong public sector serving all.** The commitment runs from the New Deal through the European social democracies to contemporary cases for digital public infrastructure and a public-interest technology.
- **Racism and colonialism are not incidental to capitalism** as it actually developed. The racial-capitalism line — Cedric Robinson, Du Bois, Eric Williams (not yet in the graph but high on the list), Wilkerson, Davis — is part of the political-economic core, not a separate department.

### On moral and religious life

- **Kindness and empathy are not sentimental** — they are political and epistemic. Simone Weil's *attention*, the capability approach (Sen and Nussbaum), the feminist ethics of care, and the civil-rights tradition of the Beloved Community all point in the same direction.
- **Unexamined privilege is pervasive.** Listening to those without political or economic voice is a core epistemic obligation. The graph is accordingly weighted toward the traditions that have done that listening well: the Black American intellectual tradition (Douglass through Baldwin through Coates), feminist thought across generations, writers who chronicle working and poor American lives.
- **Sermon-on-the-Mount Christianity matters**, even for those of us who have a complicated relation to institutional religion. The radical tradition inside Christianity — Day, Merton, Bonhoeffer, Thurman, King, Romero, Gutiérrez, Stevenson, Berry, Rauschenbusch — speaks more clearly than the respectable middle of American church life, and Black American Christianity speaks more clearly still.
- **Good and evil come intertwined.** I am more Niebuhrian than progressive-confident, more existentialist than essentialist, more pluralist than systematizing. The graph accordingly includes Niebuhr, Berlin, Bernard Williams, Flannery O'Connor, Arendt, the existentialists.
- **Wariness of institutionalized religion** paired with recognition of its capacity for social good. The figures I most trust inside Christianity are the ones working against the grain of their institutions from inside them.

### On art and the life of the mind

- **Literature and the arts are not decoration.** They are one of the ways a civilization thinks, and often the way it thinks most seriously about what doesn't fit into theoretical prose. The graph includes novelists, poets, and playwrights on the same footing as political theorists — because Morrison, Césaire, Akhmatova, Soyinka, Beckett, and Dylan are political thinkers in a register that prose argument alone cannot reach.
- **Design, architecture, and the built environment are political.** Morris, the Bauhaus, Mumford, Jane Jacobs, Christopher Alexander — the question of how we build and what we build for is continuous with the question of what kind of society we are making.

### On method

- **The short entry** — two or three paragraphs, key ideas, key works, a few secondary sources — is the unit I've found most useful. Longer entries I don't reread; much shorter ones don't leave enough to find your way back by. The graph is built to be returned to, not read through.
- **Cross-links over categories.** The most important thing about a thinker is usually who they were arguing with and who they made possible. The *influenced_by* / *influenced* / *related* fields do more work than the tags.
- **Secondary sources are named, not gestured at.** If an entry recommends a book, it names the book, the edition where it matters, and what the book is good for.

## Biases the reader should know about

- **American-centered**, on the non-European side. The graph knows more about American political and religious life than about any other national tradition, including the British one.
- **Christian-inflected, non-denominationally.** The religious parts of the graph are denser on Christianity than on Judaism, Islam, or the Asian traditions, and the Christianity is weighted toward the radical, social-gospel, liberation-theology, and contemplative lines rather than the evangelical or confessional ones.
- **Left of center**, in the American sense of that phrase. Not uniformly — conservative thinkers of real weight (MacIntyre, Burke, Oakeshott, parts of Niebuhr, Scruton) are included because they have to be — but the center of gravity is to the left.
- **Post-1800, mostly.** The ancients are present where they have to be (Aristotle, Confucius) but they are the exceptions; the graph is primarily a map of modernity and its immediate prehistory.
- **Living thinkers are thinner** than historical ones, because the shape of a living career isn't yet settled and I'm more likely to be wrong about who will matter.

## What this is for

This is a private working map I've decided to make public. I use it to find my way back into conversations I care about, to see what I'm missing, and to remember who argued what with whom. If it's useful to someone else, that's a bonus, not the purpose.

Suggestions — including "you've got X wrong" and "you're missing Y" — are welcome. The graph is a draft that will never be finished.
