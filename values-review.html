Your values, in brief

  • Philosophical: kindness and empathy are central; unexamined privilege is pervasive; listen to those without political/economic voice; racism and sexism are rampant and often invisible; capitalism may have racism/colonialism baked in; the teachings of Jesus (especially the Sermon on the Mount) matter, but modern White Christianity often doesn't exemplify them; Black American Christianity resonates more; good and evil come intertwined.

Already in the graph — your likely high-value reading list

On regulated capitalism / strong public sector

  • polanyi-karl — the "fictitious commodities" and "double movement" framework is the cleanest theoretical underwriting of "capitalism but regulated."
  • keynes-jm, welfare-state — the mid-century settlement described in your first four political bullets.
  • piketty-thomas, harvey-david, klein-naomi, giridharadas-anand — the contemporary case that unregulated capital reproduces harm.

On voice to the voiceless / unexamined privilege

  • weil-simone — attention as the central moral discipline; her theology of affliction is the closest thing I can point you to on why listening to the voiceless is a spiritual, not just political, obligation.
  • sen-amartya — the capability approach: what does it mean to take the actually-situated person seriously.
  • ellison-ralph, baldwin-james, cobb-jelani — the tradition of registering what dominant eyes won't see.
  • wollstonecraft-mary, de-beauvoir-simone, intersectionality.

On Sermon-on-the-Mount Christianity / critique of White Christianity / Black Christian resonance

  • day-dorothy, merton-thomas — two different ways of taking the Sermon literally.
  • bonhoeffer-dietrich — his "cheap grace" is almost exactly your diagnosis of White American Christianity, phrased as theology.
  • king-mlk — the graph's central bridge between Black Christianity and public action.
  • niebuhr-reinhold — the Christian realist register for "good and evil intertwined."
  • tillich-paul — "the Protestant principle: no finite form captures the infinite" is a theological critique of any church that thinks it has arrived.

On good and evil intertwined / moral complexity

  • niebuhr-reinhold again; o-connor-flannery (grace as violent intrusion into self-satisfied virtue); arendt-hannah (banality of evil); berlin-isaiah (value pluralism — incommensurable goods that can't both be fully honored).

Missing — strong candidates to add

Black American Christianity + intellectual tradition

Biggest gap given your VALUES:

  • W.E.B. Du Bois — nearly a structural omission. The Souls of Black Folk, double-consciousness, the religious texture of early Black American political thought.
  • Howard Thurman — Black mystic theologian, mentor to MLK, author of Jesus and the Disinherited (arguably the single book closest to what you describe). Essential addition.
  • James H. Cone — Black liberation theology; The Cross and the Lynching Tree explicitly argues that White American Christianity has refused to see what Black Christianity has always seen.
  • Cornel West — contemporary Black prophetic Christianity.
  • Frederick Douglass — foundational.
  • John Lewis — Beloved Community in civil-rights practice.

"Racism/colonialism baked into capitalism"

Your bullet #5 under philosophical has its own scholarly tradition:

  • Eric Williams — Capitalism and Slavery (1944), founding argument that Atlantic capitalism was built on slavery, not merely entangled with it.
  • Cedric Robinson — Black Marxism and the "racial capitalism" concept.
  • Walter Rodney — How Europe Underdeveloped Africa.
  • Isabel Wilkerson — Caste and The Warmth of Other Suns.
  • Michelle Alexander — The New Jim Crow.
  • Ta-Nehisi Coates — Between the World and Me.
  • Bryan Stevenson — Just Mercy. Christian-rooted justice advocacy in a direct Sermon-on-the-Mount lineage.

Attention to the unheard / American working lives

  • Barbara Ehrenreich — Nickel and Dimed.
  • Matthew Desmond — Evicted, Poverty, by America.
  • Howard Zinn — A People's History, history from below.

Agrarian / communitarian Christian critique of corporate capitalism

Explicit Sermon-on-the-Mount sensibility with serious political teeth:

  • Wendell Berry — essays and fiction; critique of industrial agriculture, defense of local community, lay Baptist. Probably pays off the most per entry for you.
  • Marilynne Robinson — novelist (Gilead trilogy) and essayist; American Calvinist humanism.

Plus five women

To complement the five above:

  2. Pauli Murray — civil-rights lawyer, Black feminist theorist, Episcopal priest. Sits at the crossroads of nearly every value you name.
  3. bell hooks — Black feminism, love as political practice, teaching as liberation.
  4. Isabel Wilkerson — The Warmth of Other Suns and Caste; racial capitalism through historical journalism.
  5. Jane Addams — Hull House, the American settlement movement, public-sector commitment, voice for immigrants and the urban poor.
