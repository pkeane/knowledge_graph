The Folk Revival

1930s–1960s
#music#american-thought#culture#politics

The movement — or rather, two overlapping movements — that collected, reinterpreted, and performed the traditional music of the American rural working class as a vehicle for cultural identity and political dissent. The first wave, in the 1930s and 1940s, was driven by the convergence of New Deal cultural nationalism, left-wing labor organizing, and the fieldwork of collectors like John and Alan Lomax. The second wave, in the late 1950s and early 1960s, was the one that entered the mainstream: the Kingston Trio, Joan Baez, Peter Paul and Mary, and then Bob Dylan, who blew the whole thing open and left it behind. The revival was always a paradox: an urban, educated, often middle-class audience embracing the music of rural poverty and labor struggle, sometimes with and sometimes without awareness of the distance.

The story cannot be told without Lead Belly and Woody Guthrie, who were not revivalists but originators — the people the revivalists were listening to. And it cannot be told without Harry Smith, whose Anthology of American Folk Music (1952) was the single most influential document in shaping what the revival thought folk music sounded like.

Annotated bibliography

The collectors and the archive

  • Alan Lomax, The Land Where the Blues Began (1993) — Lomax's account of his fieldwork in the Mississippi Delta; part memoir, part ethnography, part argument that the blues is one of America's great cultural achievements. Lomax's earlier field recordings for the Library of Congress (1930s–1940s) are the foundation of the archive the revival drew on.
  • Harry Smith, Anthology of American Folk Music (1952; reissued Smithsonian Folkways, 1997) — the six-LP compilation that became the revival's canon. Smith selected from commercial 78s of the 1920s and 1930s and organized them into three volumes (Ballads, Social Music, Songs). Greil Marcus called it "the founding document of the American folk revival." The Smithsonian reissue includes Smith's extraordinary liner notes.
  • Benjamin Filene, Romancing the Folk: Public Memory and American Roots Music (2000) — the best scholarly treatment of how collectors (the Lomaxes, Smith, Charles Seeger) constructed "folk music" as a category and what got included and excluded.

The originators

  • Woody Guthrie, Bound for Glory (1943) — Guthrie's autobiography; Dust Bowl migration, hobo life, radical populism. The voice that defined what "folk singer" meant for the revival generation.
  • Lead Belly — no single written text; the essential sources are the recordings (Smithsonian Folkways has the authoritative collections) and Charles Wolfe and Kip Lornell, The Life and Legend of Leadbelly (1992). Lead Belly's music crossed blues, folk, gospel, and work-song traditions and reached the revival audience through the Lomaxes' promotion and his own performances in New York.
  • Joe Klein, Woody Guthrie: A Life (1980) — the standard biography; better on the politics and the context than on the music.

The second wave and Dylan

  • Bob Dylan, Chronicles: Volume One (2004) — Dylan's memoir of arriving in New York in 1961; the Greenwich Village folk scene, the influence of Guthrie and Lead Belly, the early recordings. Selective and unreliable in the way Dylan always is, but essential.
  • Elijah Wald, Dylan Goes Electric!: Newport, Seeger, Dylan, and the Night That Split the Sixties (2015) — the 1965 Newport Folk Festival as the moment the revival cracked open; good on the politics of authenticity that the revival generated.
  • Robert Cantwell, When We Were Good: The Folk Revival (1996) — the best single-volume cultural history of the revival as a social phenomenon; takes seriously the class and cultural contradictions of the movement.
  • Ronald D. Cohen, Rainbow Quest: The Folk Music Revival and American Society, 1940–1970 (2002) — a straightforward narrative history; good on the institutional infrastructure (Folkways Records, Sing Out! magazine, the festival circuit).

The politics of authenticity

  • Greil Marcus, Invisible Republic: Bob Dylan's Basement Tapes (1997; reissued as The Old, Weird America) — not a conventional history but an argument about what the Anthology of American Folk Music meant and what Dylan did with it. Marcus traces a line from the "old, weird America" of the commercial 78s through Smith's curation to Dylan's Basement Tapes sessions.
  • Karl Hagstrom Miller, Segregating Sound: Inventing Folk and Pop Music in the Age of Jim Crow (2010) — argues that the categories "folk" and "popular" were imposed on Southern music by the recording industry and by collectors, and that the racial segregation of musical categories mirrored and reinforced the racial segregation of the Jim Crow South.
  • Marybeth Hamilton, In Search of the Blues: Black Voices, White Visions (2008) — how the idea of "authentic" blues was constructed by white collectors and critics; relevant to the revival's relationship with Black music more broadly.

The broader tradition

  • Wendell Berry, The Unsettling of America (1977) — not about the folk revival directly, but Berry's defense of agrarian culture, local knowledge, and the dignity of rural labor shares the revival's deepest commitments, stripped of the romanticization.
  • Pete Seeger and Bob Reiser, Everybody Says Freedom (1989) — Seeger's account of music in the civil rights movement; the revival's political dimension at its most consequential.
