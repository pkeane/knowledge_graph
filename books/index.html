A Reading List

Books that match the values lens of this knowledge graph and (where possible) connect to nodes already in it. A mix of essential classics you may not have read and recent works that synthesize the past well.

On inequality as threat to democracy

Five books that together make the case — now central to your VALUES — that wealth concentration and democratic decay are the same story.

  • Robert Reich, Saving Capitalism: For the Many, Not the Few (2015) — the clearest single statement of the vicious cycle. Short, direct, written for a general reader. Start here.
  • Jacob Hacker & Paul Pierson, Winner-Take-All Politics (2010) — the political-science companion: how the American rules were rewritten, by whom, over what period.
  • Hacker & Pierson, Let Them Eat Tweets (2020) — their shorter, sharper update on "plutocratic populism" — why an economically unpopular agenda needs ethno-nationalist distraction to survive.
  • Thomas Piketty, Capital in the Twenty-First Century (2014) — the empirical spine. Long, but the introduction and Part IV alone repay the effort.
  • Anand Giridharadas, Winners Take All (2018) — on how philanthropy launders the arrangement. Readable, vivid, uncomfortable in the best way.
  • David Lay Williams, The Greatest of All Plagues (2024) — the intellectual-history backbone the others assume but don't supply: Plato, Jesus, Hobbes, Rousseau, Smith, Mill, and Marx all reading wealth concentration as a primary political danger. The book that lets you say "this concern is as old as political thought itself" and mean it precisely.

On digital public infrastructure and platform power

The new VALUES bullet on non-partisan digital services opens a cluster where the strongest books are quite recent.

  • Shoshana Zuboff, The Age of Surveillance Capitalism (2019) — the fullest theoretical account. Long and dense; the first hundred pages are the load-bearing ones.
  • Tim Wu, The Curse of Bigness (2018) — short polemic; the best one-sitting introduction to the New Brandeisian antitrust argument.
  • Cory Doctorow, The Internet Con (2023) — under 200 pages; the clearest popular statement of the "interoperability and right of exit" case. Reads like a long essay.
  • Mariana Mazzucato, The Entrepreneurial State (2013) — listed again here because it is the single best book on the public origins of the technologies the platforms enclosed.

Six classics, each short

  1. Howard Thurman, Jesus and the Disinherited (1949) — under 100 pages. The single book that maps closest to your VALUES; MLK reportedly carried it on the road. If you read only one thing on this list, this one.
  2. W.E.B. Du Bois, The Souls of Black Folk (1903) — fourteen essays mixing sociology, memoir, and lyric meditation. Foundation of American Black political thought; the chapter on the sorrow songs is a music essay you'll never forget.
  3. James Baldwin, The Fire Next Time (1963) — two essays, ~120 pages. Devastatingly clear on race and on what White American Christianity has refused to see. Reads in one sitting.
  4. Reinhold Niebuhr, The Irony of American History (1952) — the canonical Christian-realist statement on good and evil intertwined. Short, hard, durable.
  5. Wendell Berry, Hannah Coulter (2004) — short novel. Berry's agrarian Christianity in narrative rather than essay form; quieter than the essays and easier to enter through.
  6. Marilynne Robinson, Gilead (2004) — Pulitzer; a small American masterpiece. An aging Iowa Congregationalist preacher writing to his young son. Calvinist humanism rendered as fiction. (Robinson is on the wishlist but not yet in the graph.)

Four recent works that synthesize well

  1. Bryan Stevenson, Just Mercy (2014) — the indispensable contemporary book on the American carceral state. Built around the Walter McMillian case; in a Sermon-on-the-Mount lineage but by way of Alabama death row.
  2. Isabel Wilkerson, The Warmth of Other Suns (2010) — fifteen years of reporting on the Great Migration through three life stories. One of the best American nonfiction books of the century, on a subject most American history misses.
  3. Elizabeth Anderson, Private Government (2017) — about 100 pages of lectures plus respondents. The argument that the modern American workplace is de facto authoritarian and that classical liberalism, properly understood, ought to be horrified.
  4. Heather McGhee, The Sum of Us (2021) — accessible, vivid, the single best contemporary book on how racism costs everyone (the "drained pool" framing). Reads like long-form journalism; argues like serious political analysis. (McGhee is not yet in the graph.)

One wildcard worth your attention

  1. Adam Tooze, Crashed (2018) — 700 pages on the 2008 financial crisis and the decade after; the most ambitious recent attempt to write the political economy of crisis as a single coherent story. Hard going but worth it; it's the book to read after Stiglitz, Krugman, and Minsky to see how the crisis actually unfolded across the world economy. (Tooze is not yet in the graph.)

Fiction — in the same values-cluster

Novels (and stories) that do the philosophical and political work of the graph in another idiom. The two novels listed in the classics section above (Berry's Hannah Coulter, Marilynne Robinson's Gilead) also belong here.

  1. Walker Percy, The Moviegoer (1961) — National Book Award. Binx Bolling, a New Orleans stockbroker, living in what Percy called "everydayness" and undertaking a quiet, Kierkegaardian search he can't quite name. One of the clearest American expressions of existentialist themes in fiction; a short, strange, funny, permanent book.
  2. Robertson Davies, The Manticore (1972) — the second of the Deptford Trilogy. A Canadian lawyer undergoes Jungian analysis in Zurich after his father's violent death; the novel is the analysis, rendered with unusual technical accuracy. Best read after Fifth Business (1970), which sets up the world, but can stand alone.
  3. Flannery O'Connor, A Good Man Is Hard to Find (1955) — short stories. O'Connor's Catholic imagination in its compressed, violent, unforgettable form. Grace as intrusion rather than comfort.
  4. Toni Morrison, Beloved (1987) — Pulitzer. Built around the historical case of Margaret Garner. The great American novel of the afterlives of slavery; formally daring, ethically unsparing. By broad consensus one of the major American novels of the 20th century.
  5. Ralph Ellison, Invisible Man (1952) — mid-century American masterpiece. The unnamed Black narrator's picaresque from the South through Harlem; prose that never settles into one register for long. Among the handful of essential postwar American novels.
  6. James Baldwin, Go Tell It on the Mountain (1953) — Baldwin's first novel, semi-autobiographical, set in a Harlem Pentecostal church across one Saturday night. The novel in which his later essayistic concerns — Black Christianity, the father, inherited sin, the claim of love — first take full form.
  7. Virginia Woolf, To the Lighthouse (1927) — her formal masterpiece. The middle section "Time Passes" — war, death, and the decay of the summer house recorded in ten elliptical pages between two domestic scenes — is one of the great experiments of modernist prose.
  8. William Faulkner, Absalom, Absalom! (1936) — Faulkner's most ambitious book. Four narrators try to make sense of Thomas Sutpen's rise and ruin in antebellum Mississippi. The great American novel of how the past will not stay past; race, memory, and inheritance as the subject matter of the country itself.
  9. Albert Camus, The Plague (1947) — a plague descends on Oran and the book tests, unsentimentally and without theological consolation, what decency looks like under conditions that admit no triumph. Camus's most durable work of fiction.
  10. Omar El Akkad, What Strange Paradise (2021) — Giller Prize. Short alternating chapters between a drowning refugee boat and the small island receiving its survivors. Contemporary, sharply made, morally unflinching.
  11. Joan Didion, Play It As It Lays (1970) — her best novel. California, anomie, the hollowed-out interior life of a particular American 1960s. Short, exact, terrifying in the way Didion at her best is terrifying.

Three one-month plans

Month of VALUES core: Thurman → Baldwin → Niebuhr → Stevenson → McGhee. Five short books, total under a thousand pages, that together rehearse most of your VALUES.md from inside the traditions that hold them.

Month of inequality & democracy: Reich's Saving Capitalism → Hacker & Pierson's Let Them Eat Tweets → Giridharadas's Winners Take All → Wu's The Curse of Bigness → Doctorow's The Internet Con. Five short, pointed books that walk you from the political economy of inequality to the platform logic that amplifies it.

Month in fiction: Percy's Moviegoer → O'Connor's stories → Baldwin's Go Tell It on the Mountain → Morrison's Beloved → Davies' Fifth Business + The Manticore. Same commitments, rendered as narrative.
